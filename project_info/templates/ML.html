GHG Emissions - The Unlikely Connections


The analysis of Pennsylvania proved what many likely expected: the Commonwealth is among one of the worst contributors of CO2 emissions across the country. But maybe you didn't know just how aggressively PA contributes to the overall issue for the U.S. wWe wanted to put numbers to that conversation. We wanted you to know so that when you take action, you know the reason.


Thinking about what we didn't know, as a team we wondered if there was anything else contributing to CO2 emissions that was unseen. Community organizers across the nation will tell you that climate injustice is deeply tied to other forms of injustice and intricately so. Given the amount of people that have access to public programs in a county, can a machine tell if the CO2 emissions will be bad? Given information on marginalized communities like folks of color and folks with disabilities, can a machine determine if CO2 levels are within "acceptable" standards? We wanted to prove it.


Machine Learning (ML) is an incredibly powerful tool given it's predictive and classifying capability. So we sought out to aggregate small sets of social data (healthcare, statistics on policing, land ownership, public program access, etc.) for each county in the Commonwealth and determine if ML could be employed to predict if a given county's CO2 emissions were likely to be acceptable or not.


We used two different classifiers for our study.One was a land based classifier that characterized the county's CO2 emissions as above or below a national level for average CO2 emissions per area of land. The other is a rate based classifier that used the change between the two most recent years of data we had access to for a given county to identify whether CO2 emissions were going up or down. Using these two classifiers, we fed this information to 8 ML algorithms available for from Python's Scikit Learn library.


We wanted to use a high number of classification algorithms with one goal in mind: if we throw these counties' social data outcomes and classifiers at multiple ML algorithms and these models score above 50% with little to no fine-tuning, then there certainly must be correlations between social outcomes and climate change. Further, this suggests to us that addressing other serious social issues can actually help chip away at the climate crisis. Of course, a significantly deeper analysis would need to be done on each individual dataset and the models would need functional fine-tuning to understand the degree of correlation.



Our Approach & Analysis

1. In general, the land based classifier scored higher than the rate based classifier consistently. What this means is that across all models, the selected Python supervised learning algorithms were able to predict that CO2 levels would be above/below the national standard if given seemingly unrelated social data sets. Sometimes, that prediction score was a high as 75% or even 81% depending on the data used for predictions (see Age data).


For the purpose of the site, the default datasets depicted are Education and Public Program. But, feel free to use the checkboxes on the right-hand side of the bar graph to take a closer look at a dataset in which you have more interest!


2. In general, the land based classifier scored higher than 50% across all 21 data sets with nearly every single type of classification model employed. There were some unique cases where the model scored well below 50% at about 37% (see Farm data).


3. Although none of these models were scoring above 90% in their predictive capability, it's important to note that these models have little to no fine tuning. We believe this means that there is truth to the anecdote that social injustices/issues and the climate crisis are intertwined. We wonder if remedying these other social injustices may in turn help to chip away at climate change as well. Of course, specific fine tuning for each model should be done per dataset to significantly improve or identify the limits of predictive capability.
