Pennsylvania GHG Emissions & Air Quality by County


The Clear Air Act requires the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) to set National Air Ambient Quality Standards (NAAQS) for six principle pollutants which can be harmful to public and the environmental health1.


Based on the dataset from the Enterprise Data Dissemination Informatics Exchange (EDDIE)2, 2010 was the worst year for air quality in PA and since 2014, has trended around 200 days over the standard set by the NAAQS (see PA Air Quality, 2009-2018 chart). From 2009-2018, counties with the worst air quality are Philadelphia and Allegheny county (see Air Quality by County, 2009-2018 chart).


Breakdown of Carbon Emissions


Utilizing the greenhouse gas emissions from large facilities dataset3, from 2009-2018 Indiana and Armstrong county consistently had the largest overall carbon emissions (in 2019, nearly 20M and 12M metric tons respectively). When exploring the facility emissions at the county level, it became apparent that the bulk of carbon emissions weren’t isolated to one specific facility, but rather multiple facilities’ parent company. For instance, in 2019, Arclight Capital LLC (an energy conglomerate) accounted for nearly 53.4% all CO2 emissions in Indiana County and 95.8% of all emissions in Armstrong County.

Lastly, the GHG Emissions3 vs. Air Pollution chart2 (2018) depicts the limitations of the data and the NAAQS measurement standards; out of 67 PA counties, 13 counties reported zero (0) air quality days of the standard and the 27 counties reported nothing. Furthermore, variation in population between counties can have a significant impact on the number of person-days above the standards. The number of days above the NAAQS x number of people = number of person-days above set standard. For example, Indiana and Armstrong have the highest overall carbon emissions but a small population size; and thus, their person-days above the standard is only at 2 days.
